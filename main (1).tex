% 3. ABSTRACT
\begin{abstract}
This practicum addresses the challenge of predicting 3-year Return on Investment (ROI) in the Colorado residential housing market. By synthesizing longitudinal data from Zillow, the U.S. Census Bureau, and the NCES, the study evaluates the non-linear correlation between community infrastructure and capital appreciation. A Random Forest Regressor was trained on 25,907 entries, achieving an $R^2$ of 0.85 and a Mean Absolute Error (MAE) of 7.24\%. Validation on Frederick, CO, confirms that high school density provides a protective safety floor, allowing for data-driven risk mitigation.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction/Background}
The Colorado real estate market has seen significant appreciation, yet remains susceptible to localized volatility that traditional models fail to capture. This research moves beyond standard univariate price forecasting by integrating socio-economic stabilizers. The motivation is to provide a "forward-looking windshield" for investors, moving away from a reliance on historical price trends which often ignore the underlying community infrastructure that maintains value during market corrections.

\section{Problem Statement}
Real estate valuation currently suffers from "univariate bias," where future gains are predicted solely on past movement. This fails to account for infrastructure density, which serves as a stabilizing force. This project aims to quantify the relationship between community features and ROI to create a standardized risk-management framework that protects investor capital.

\section{Methodology/Proposed Approach}
The project utilizes a Random Forest Regressor, an ensemble learning method that aggregates 1,000 decision trees to find complex patterns. This architecture was selected over Linear Regression because real estate drivers—like the relationship between income and school quality—are rarely linear. By using an ensemble approach, we reduce the risk of overfitting while gaining deep insights into "Feature Importance."
